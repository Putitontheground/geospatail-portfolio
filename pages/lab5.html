Lab 5 - Raster Analysis

Agroforestry Suitability Analysis

1. Define the Problem

The project aims to identify suitable land for different agroforestry crops in Fiji's Sigatoka Valley, focusing exclusively on soil-related and environmental factors. This clear definition helps target the study's efforts directly towards the factors that influence crop viability, ensuring that the analysis is relevant and manageable.

2. Structure the Problem

To structure the problem, we identified the necessary data layers based on the cultivation criteria for the crops. These criteria were compiled into a reference table, outlining the GIS datasets needed. This step is crucial as it maps out the variables and data structures required for the spatial analysis, ensuring that all essential factors are considered.

3. Acquire and Prepare Data

The data was sourced from various established databases and resources. The preparation involved several key processes:

Converting Vector Data to Raster

This was essential for handling soil data, which originally existed in vector format. The conversion facilitates easier manipulation and analysis in GIS.

Generating Slope Data from Elevation

Using digital elevation models, we converted elevation data to slope data, crucial for evaluating terrain suitability.

Creating Climatic Rasters via IDW Interpolation

We interpolated point data for rainfall and temperature to create continuous raster surfaces, providing a detailed environmental overview.

These preparation steps were fundamental to ensuring that the GIS data was ready for detailed spatial analysis, setting the stage for our next analysis phase which was to combine the three maps to find areas which all crops could be grown.

Spatial Analysis of Crop Suitability

The spatial analysis provided clear insights into the distribution and suitability of land for the specified crops:

Vesi (Intsia bijuga):

This crop showed the least suitability, with only 7,651 hectares being appropriate for its cultivation. This indicates limited growth potential for Vesi possibly due to it's restrictive requirements for good growth and the regions varied environmental conditions.

Cutnut (Barringtonia edulis):

Demonstrated a higher level of suitability, with 116,112 hectares deemed suitable. This suggests that the region's environmental conditions are favorable for cutnut cultivation, potentially making it a key agricultural asset.

Breadfruit (Artocarpus altilis):

Had a substantial proportion of suitable land, with 55,580 hectares. This indicates that breadfruit can thrive over a wide area, likely due to it's tolerance for growing at higher elevations

Key Observation:

A noteworthy observation from the analysis is that the total area suitable for all three crops corresponds precisely to the area suitable for Vesi (Intsia bijuga), suggesting that the specific conditions required for Vesi cultivation are the limiting factor for multi-crop cultivation in this region.
