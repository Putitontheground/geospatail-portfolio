Coastline Paradox and Map Scale

The coastline paradox and map scale are closely related. The idea of the coastline paradox says the length of a coastline isn't easily measurable as it seems to grow infinitely as you measure it in smaller units. This happens because coastlines have a fractal nature, meaning they show similar patterns at different scales.

Map scale compares a map's distance to actual earth distance, a larger scale map shows a smaller area in more detail, while a smaller scale map covers a bigger area with less detail.

Smaller scale maps (covering bigger areas) when related to coastlines, look smoother and straighter, making the distance seem shorter when measured. As you follow more straight lines you would end up underestimating coastline length.

As you zoom in to a large scale more details such as bays, inlets and peninsulas become apparent. Measuring the coastline to now include those details requires following the curve rather than straight line, making it seem longer.

The coastline paradox shows how map scale affects the perceived length of a coastline. The measured length doesn't show the actual length as it depends on the scale and the detail level.

The Robinson Projection

In the 1960s, Arthur H. Robinson, a geography professor, developed a map projection that became more popular than the Mercator projection for world maps. It aimed to address the distortions of the Mercator projection, offering a more realistic representation.

The Robinson projection replaced the Mercator projection as the preferred choice for world maps by major publishers like Rand McNally and National Geographic.

It maintains a balance between area and shape distortions, with less severe distortions than the Mercator projection. The Robinson projection uses a pseudo-cylindrical shape, with the Equator serving as the standard parallel. Unlike the Mercator projection, which has evenly spaced lines of latitude and longitude across the map, only the central meridian (longitude) in the Robinson projection is straight; all other lines of longitude curve, with increasing curvature farther from the central meridian.
